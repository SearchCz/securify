Observer Focus

Every observer begins with a Focus.

Focus defines the condition the observer watches.

When that condition becomes true, the observer activates.

Activation is reflected directly in Indigo by turning the observer device On.

An activated observer indicates only one thing:

The condition this observer watches currently exists.

Activation does not imply that the homeowner will be notified.

Notification depends upon the observer’s Authority and Response configuration, which are described separately.

⸻

Choosing a Focus

Every observer watches either:

* a specific checkpoint, or
* the home as a whole.

Monitoring the home as a whole allows observers to respond to changing conditions anywhere in the home, while monitoring a specific checkpoint allows responses tailored to an individual door, sensor, room, or other point of interest.

⸻

Focus Conditions

An observer can activate in response to three different kinds of change.

Alert Classification

The most common focus is an Alert Classification.

The observer activates when a checkpoint—or the home as a whole—reaches a homeowner-selected Alert Classification.

For example:

* Notify when the Front Door reaches Alert Classification 3.
* Watch for any condition in the home reaching Alert Classification 5.

This is the primary way observers recognize meaningful conditions.

⸻

Increasing Significance

Sometimes the important event is not reaching a particular Alert Classification, but simply becoming more significant.

An observer may therefore activate whenever a checkpoint’s significance increases.

This allows homeowners to respond immediately whenever a condition worsens, regardless of its current Alert Classification.

⸻

Decreasing Significance

Observers may also activate when significance decreases.

This is particularly useful for recognizing improving conditions.

For example:

* a garage door has been closed
* a water leak has been resolved
* a vulnerable condition has become secure

These observers provide confirmation that a condition has improved without requiring additional automation.

⸻

Multiple Observers

A single checkpoint may be watched by any number of observers.

Each observer defines its own Focus, Authority, and Response, allowing the same changing condition to be interpreted in different ways.

For example, a garage door checkpoint might have several observers:

* One observer activates when the checkpoint reaches Alert Classification 2, announcing that the garage door has been left open.
* A second observer activates when the checkpoint reaches Alert Classification 4, sending an email if the condition becomes more serious.
* A third observer activates whenever the checkpoint’s significance decreases, announcing that the garage door has been secured.

Although all three observers watch the same checkpoint, each has its own purpose and operates completely independently of the others.

Observers do not escalate.

Conditions escalate.

If a homeowner wishes to respond differently as a condition becomes more significant, they simply create additional observers, each watching for the condition they care about and responding in the manner they consider appropriate.

This separation allows Securify to remain predictable, flexible, and easy to understand.

⸻

Activation

When an observer’s focus becomes true, the observer activates.

Its Indigo device turns On, making the observed condition visible throughout Indigo.

The observer remains active for as long as its focus continues to be satisfied.

When the observed condition no longer exists, the observer automatically turns Off.

This On/Off state is independent of notifications.

An observer may be active without ever sending a response.

Likewise, a response may occur only after additional Authority requirements—such as persistence or gating—have been satisfied.

Because observers are ordinary Indigo devices, homeowners may also create their own Indigo triggers, conditions, and Action Groups that react to an observer’s activation or deactivation.

⸻

Focus Is Awareness

Focus answers a single question:

“What condition am I interested in?”

Nothing more.

It does not decide whether the condition has persisted long enough.

It does not determine whether the homeowner should currently be disturbed.

It simply identifies the presence of a condition worthy of observation.

Authority determines whether that condition is currently eligible to produce a response.

Response determines how the homeowner is informed.

Together, these three responsibilities allow observers to remain simple, predictable, and highly reusable.
